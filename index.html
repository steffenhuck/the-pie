The Pie

a game of cuts and appetites

A guest has come to dinner, and you have baked identical pies — cherries here, almonds there, cake everywhere. You know the guest is one of three palates, each precise to the point. You do not know which.

Each course, cut one wedge: drag a finger around the rim — or set two fingers on the pie at once. A cut offers a choice — wedge against rest — and nothing reaches the table until you put that choice to the guest, so redraw as often as you like. Once served, he takes whichever piece he scores higher; if the pieces tie, he shrugs and you see that too.

His choices are all you will ever learn — and the table keeps its own counsel: no palate is marked off while you play. The reckoning is yours; only the verdict shows who survived. Cut where the palates disagree — a piece every palate would take teaches you nothing.

Early dinners keep the toppings tidy. Later ones mingle them — and a pie that looks busier may say less.

When you are ready, name the palate — or rule that no cut of this pie can tell the survivors apart. A proven limit is worth as much as a naming. Haste is worth nothing; neither is surrender while a telling cut remains.

Pies don’t keep. You have only so many courses.
